## Summary<a name="summary"></a>

- **MiCA removes USDT spot from the account entirely.** In a Binance-EEA operation, trading occurs in USDC, EURI, and EUR, plus crypto/crypto pairs (ETH/BTC etc.). USDT can still be held, deposited, withdrawn, and converted — but not traded on spot. This is an exchange/CASP obligation, not a personal legal restriction on the individual trader (Section 2).
- **VIP2 status turns the forced USDC move into a fee edge.** VIP2 (reached via the 100-BNB holding path) gives 0.08% maker / 0.10% taker, dropping to 0.06% / 0.075% with the 25% BNB deduction, and falls within the VIP 2–9 band eligible for Binance's rotating zero-fee USDC promotions. Baseline ~12 bps round-trip on normal USDC pairs; ~0 bps on current zero-fee majors.
- **The chosen strategy is appropriate.** A cross-sectional ML ranker (LightGBM/MLP on Alpha158) over ~20 USDC pairs, predicting 1–3-day forward returns, daily-rebalanced, long/cash, executed with maker limit orders, with a BTC-trend regime overlay. This avoids head-to-head competition with HFT — which is in any case irrelevant at hours-to-days — and aligns with the fee structure.
- **The starting data frequency is daily (1-day) bars.** This matches the 1–3-day horizon and daily rebalance, keeps Alpha158 factor semantics native, and minimizes both turnover and overfitting surface. The binding requirement is history length: one year is a single regime, so 3–5 years are needed. Modeling uses the long, freely available USDT-quoted history (an economically identical series), with execution on the USDC pair. Trade/aggTrade data are not required for the daily signal.
- **A higher-frequency (15-minute) extension is deferred.** Its intended shape — 15-min features and triple-barrier labels, hours-to-days holding, execution gated to an hourly rebalance with a no-trade band — and the triggers for adopting it are set out in Section 14.
- **Realistic expectation:** roughly 1.0–2.0 gross Sharpe in the literature; 0.5–1.2 net after fees and mid-cap slippage is a more reasonable plan, with 15–30% drawdowns and stretches underperforming a simple BTC hold. Most retail algo strategies lose money or fail to beat buy-and-hold; the first year is best treated as an education expense.

## 1. The Regulatory Frame (MiCA) — Tradeable and Non-Tradeable Assets<a name="1-the-regulatory-frame-mica-%E2%80%94-tradeable-and-non-tradeable-assets"></a>

The Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation became fully applicable at the end of 2024, with stablecoin provisions taking effect mid-2024. Under MiCA, only stablecoins whose issuer holds an EU authorization (as an e-money token or asset-referenced token) may be offered to EEA users by a regulated platform.

Binance acted on this by **delisting nine non-compliant stablecoins for EEA users effective 31 March 2025** — including USDT, DAI, TUSD, FDUSD, USDP, AEUR, UST, USTC, and PAXG (Cointelegraph, Jan 2026; Digital Watch Observatory, Mar 2025). The delisting has held into 2026.

Concretely, for an EEA account:

- **USDT spot pairs do not exist.** A BTC/USDT order cannot be placed on Binance-EEA.
- **USDT and the other delisted coins can still be held, deposited, withdrawn, and converted** — MiCA does not prohibit custody or transfer, only the *offering* of trading and services by the platform (DailyCoin, Jul 2025). Binance Convert remains available for moving USDT into USDC.
- **Compliant venues that remain:** USDC and EURI (Eurite) stablecoin pairs, EUR fiat pairs, and any crypto/crypto pair (which involves no stablecoin and is unaffected).

As of early 2026, USDT, DAI, FDUSD, TUSD and several others still lack MiCA authorization, and Tether has signaled no intention to pursue it; the authorized set is dominated by USDC and a cluster of euro tokens. The effect across the EEA has been a migration of trader float into USDC and euro-denominated stablecoins.

**Bottom line:** USDC is the primary quote asset by regulation, not by choice — and as Section 8 shows, this aligns with the VIP2 fee advantage.

## 2. Personal Legal Exposure of Holding or Trading USDT in Germany<a name="2-personal-legal-exposure-of-holding-or-trading-usdt-in-germany"></a>

Treating this as an exchange-level matter rather than a trader-level one is correct.

MiCA regulates two parties: the **issuers** of stablecoins and the **Crypto-Asset Service Providers** (exchanges, custodians, brokers). It does not criminalize or penalize an individual retail person for holding, receiving, or transferring a non-compliant stablecoin. No provision makes it an offense for a private individual to hold USDT in a wallet or move it on-chain.

What MiCA does is place the compliance burden on the *platform*: Binance may not **offer** USDT to EEA users — no listing, trading pairs, yield, or margin. That is why USDT spot disappeared from EEA accounts. This reflects Binance discharging its obligation, not a restriction on an otherwise-legal activity by the individual.

Accordingly:

- **Holding, depositing, withdrawing, and converting USDT** remain legitimate for a private individual; Binance still supports custody and Convert for it.
- **Trading USDT spot on Binance-EEA** is simply unavailable, because the venue does not carry the product — not an offense on the trader's part.

One boundary merits stating plainly: the clean, compliant path is to use USDC on Binance-EEA. Attempting to restore USDT *trading* by circumventing the geo-restriction (VPN, a non-EEA account) would breach Binance's terms of service and risk the account; it is also unnecessary, since VIP2 status makes USDC the cheaper venue regardless. This roadmap assumes no such circumvention.

**Tax footnote (general information, not advice):** German tax treatment of crypto is independent of the stablecoin used. Gains on crypto held as private assets fall under §23 EStG (private sale transactions), with the one-year holding rule and the annual exemption threshold. A daily-rebalanced strategy generates many short-term taxable events, and per-lot FIFO accounting becomes burdensome at scale. A *Steuerberater* familiar with crypto should be consulted before going live. This roadmap is not legal or tax advice; the regulatory-versus-personal distinction is well established, but a specific tax position requires a professional.

## 3. Profitability Tradeoff — Major Coins vs. New/Volatile Coins<a name="3-profitability-tradeoff-%E2%80%94-major-coins-vs-newvolatile-coins"></a>

The real question at $10K and hours-to-days is which universe offers the most risk-adjusted edge net of fees and slippage that a single retail Python developer can capture.

**Major coins (BTC, ETH, BNB, SOL, XRP):**

- *Liquidity/slippage*: effectively unlimited depth for $10K — fills at the touch, spreads ≤1 bp on the top pairs. A $10K account is a rounding error.
- *Efficiency*: intensely arbitraged; short-horizon mean-reversion is dominated by HFT — but only at sub-minute timescales. At hours-to-days the relevant competition is swing traders and slow systematic funds, not co-located market makers.
- *Fee drag*: at the ~12 bps round-trip below, a 100–200 bps swing gives up 6–12% of gross to fees — meaningful.
- *Verdict*: low-edge, low-noise anchors. Edge here must be systematic (features, regime detection), not execution-based.

**Newer/lower-cap coins:**

- *Volatility as opportunity*: 5–15% daily ranges make fees irrelevant relative to the move.
- *Depth*: on a coin doing $5–20M/day, a $1–2k order slips ≤10 bps; below ~$5M/day, slippage dominates.
- *Manipulation*: pump-and-dump is pervasive in sub-$50M-cap coins and drives most apparent "edge."
- *Listing/delisting risk*: Binance delisting cadence is heavy — roughly 20–50 spot pairs per quarter through 2024–2025 (49 pairs in Q1 2025 alone). A delisted coin held in inventory can drop 50–90% before liquidation.
- *Verdict*: pure new-coin trading is statistically dominated by manipulation and survivorship-biased backtests.

**The sweet spot: liquid mid-caps.** Coins ranked roughly 10–50 by market cap, with $50M+ daily Binance volume, offer enough volatility that fees are negligible relative to expected move, enough depth for clean $10K fills, enough cross-sectional dispersion for an ML ranker, and *less* institutional crowding than BTC/ETH (the top five absorb most institutional flow). The basket should be weighted here.

**Strategy dependence:** cross-sectional momentum and mean-reversion both work best on liquid mid-caps; pairs/cointegration works on correlated mid-caps within a sector; market-making's only retail-viable niche is illiquid pairs, where rebate-advantaged firms still win.

## 4. Quote Leg — USDC vs. EUR/EURI vs. Crypto/Crypto<a name="4-quote-leg-%E2%80%94-usdc-vs-eureuri-vs-cryptocrypto"></a>

With USDT off the table, the real choice is among USDC, euro legs, and crypto/crypto pairs.

**USDC pairs — the primary execution venue.** USDC is MiCA-compliant (issued in the EU via Circle's authorized entity) and is the deepest compliant stablecoin venue on Binance-EEA. Order books on the majors (BTC, ETH, SOL, BNB, XRP) are solid for a $10K account. On the Tier-3 names and memecoins, USDC depth can be materially thinner than the old USDT books were, so the live order book must be checked before any backtest fill assumption is trusted.

**EUR and EURI — a useful sleeve.** EUR fiat pairs and EURI (Eurite) are compliant and allow settlement in euros, removing USD/EUR drift from P&L where the underlying capital is euro-denominated. Binance has also run EEA-specific zero-fee promotions on EUR/USDC. A small allocation is worthwhile for diversification of stablecoin-issuer risk.

**Crypto/crypto pairs (ETH/BTC, SOL/ETH) — analytical instruments.** These involve no stablecoin and are unaffected by MiCA. ETH/BTC is the most liquid crypto/crypto pair globally; beyond it, depth drops fast. Their value is cointegration research: a ratio series makes stationarity visible in a way that constructing a synthetic ratio from two stablecoin pairs cannot.

**The history trick that resolves the USDC short-history problem.** USDC pairs for many coins were listed *later* than the USDT equivalents, and EEA-specific USDC liquidity is recent, so USDC price history is often too short for a multi-regime backtest. Because the series being modeled is the *coin's price*, and BTC/USDC ≈ BTC/USDT minus a tiny, mean-reverting basis (a few bps):

> Model and backtest on the long, deep, freely downloadable **USDT-quoted** history; execute on the **USDC** pair. The economic series is effectively identical; only the quote leg differs at the margin. The small USDC/USDT basis can be carried as a minor cost term where precision is wanted.

## 5. Strategy Family Recommendation<a name="5-strategy-family-recommendation"></a>

**Is the institutional-HFT competition concern even valid at hours-to-days? Largely no.** HFT competition bites at sub-second to minute scales (queue position, latency arbitrage). At hours-to-days the relevant competition is slow: discretionary swing traders, capacity-constrained mid-frequency funds, and other (mostly losing) retail algos. The actual risks, in order: (1) fees, (2) backtest overfitting, (3) execution slippage on thin names, (4) survivorship bias, (5) regime change.

| Family                                   | Feasibility @ $10K                               | HFT/institutional competition | Qlib fit                             | Verdict                              |
| ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------ |
| Market-making (spread capture)           | Low (needs co-location, sub-ms latency, rebates) | Extreme                       | Poor                                 | **No**                               |
| Triangular / intra-exchange arbitrage    | Low (latency-bound; spreads close in ms)         | High                          | Poor                                 | **No**                               |
| Statistical arb / pairs / mean-reversion | High                                             | Low at this timescale         | **Excellent**                        | **Yes — feed into the ranker**       |
| Cross-sectional ML factor strategies     | High                                             | Low–medium                    | **Excellent** (Qlib's design center) | **Yes — primary**                    |
| Momentum / trend-following               | High                                             | Medium                        | Good (Alpha158 has momentum factors) | **Yes — secondary / regime overlay** |

**Why the chosen approach is correct.** A cross-sectional ML ranker creates *relative* signal — it ranks which of the ~20 coins will outperform the others — so it does not require the market to rise, and it is the workflow Qlib was engineered for. Because shorting is unavailable on spot, it becomes a **long/cash** strategy: hold the top-K equal-weighted, sit in USDC when model confidence is low. A BTC-trend regime filter scales gross exposure down in bear and chop regimes.

**The MiCA situation reinforces this design.** When the model signals "stay flat," capital is parked in USDC — the now-native, compliant quote asset. The maker-limit execution is precisely what the zero-fee USDC promotions reward. The pieces fit.

**Where pairs/cointegration belongs.** It works in published research, but cointegration in crypto is unstable across regimes. Cointegration-discovered relationships are best used as *features* feeding the cross-sectional model (for example, a coin's deviation from its cointegrating partner), not as a standalone strategy. Promotion to a separate sleeve is warranted only when ≥3 pairs in the basket pass a rolling Augmented Engle-Granger test.

## 6. Data Requirements — Frequency, History, and Trade vs. aggTrade<a name="6-data-requirements-%E2%80%94-frequency-history-and-trade-vs-aggtrade"></a>

**Starting frequency: daily (1-day) bars.** Daily bars are the chosen starting frequency, and the correct one for this strategy. They match the 1–3-day forecast horizon and the daily rebalance; they keep Alpha158's factor semantics native (its windows are counted in bars, so on daily data a "20-period" momentum is genuinely 20 days); and they minimize both turnover and the overfitting surface that finer bars introduce. Starting daily also keeps the first build simple enough to get correct end-to-end before complexity is added. A higher-frequency (15-minute) extension is documented in Section 14, to be considered only after the daily baseline is validated and live.

**History length is the binding requirement.** One year of data is essentially one market regime; a model trained and validated within a single regime overfits to it and tends to fail when the regime turns — and crypto's turns are violent. A meaningful backtest spans 3–5 years including at least one bull, one bear, and chop, ideally covering the LUNA (May 2022) and FTX (Nov 2022) stress events, so that behavior is observed when correlations converge to one. Binance klines are free via the public data portal (data.binance.vision) back to listing, so extending history costs only disk and ingestion time. At daily resolution the panel is small — roughly 3–5 years × 20 instruments × ~365 bars, on the order of tens of thousands of rows — so compute and storage are trivial.

**The history trick (long USDT history, USDC execution).** USDC pairs for many coins were listed later than the USDT equivalents, so USDC price history is often too short for a multi-regime backtest. Because the series being modeled is the coin's price, and BTC/USDC ≈ BTC/USDT minus a small mean-reverting basis, the resolution is to model and backtest on the long, deep, freely downloadable USDT-quoted daily history and execute on the USDC pair. The economic series is effectively identical; the small USDC/USDT basis can be carried as a minor cost term if precision is wanted.

**Trade / aggTrade data are not required for the daily signal.** For all three families (stat-arb/pairs, cross-sectional ML, momentum) the signal is entirely OHLCV-derivable: Alpha158 is OHLCV-based; momentum and mean-reversion need returns and volume; cointegration needs price series. Daily OHLCV therefore suffices for signal research. Trade/aggTrade data add execution realism — reconstructing actual fills, estimating slippage, and validating maker-fill probability. At a daily rebalance, a passive limit order has the better part of a day to fill, so maker fills are comparatively forgiving and tick-level validation is optional at this stage. A representative aggTrades sample can be pulled during pre-live validation (Stage 3–4) to confirm slippage and fill assumptions; it becomes essential only when moving to higher frequency (Section 14).

**Labeling and cross-validation.** Decisions are taken once per day (features as of 00:00 UTC, forecasting the next 1–3 days). A 1–3-day forward label still overlaps its one-to-two neighbors, so leakage across the train/test boundary remains a concern: **purged k-fold cross-validation with an embargo** (López de Prado, Ch. 7) is required even at daily frequency, with the embargo sized to cover the label horizon plus feature lookback. Combinatorial purged CV is preferable where compute allows, and the deflated Sharpe ratio should be applied when many variants are tested.

## 7. The 20-Pair Starting Basket (USDC-Centric)<a name="7-the-20-pair-starting-basket-usdc-centric"></a>

Standard tiered selection logic, re-quoted into USDC since USDT spot is unavailable, plus a crypto/crypto anchor and an optional EEA fiat sleeve. **For modeling, the USDT-quoted history of these coins is used; for execution, the USDC pair is traded.**

**Tier 1 — Majors / capacity anchors (5):**
`BTCUSDC`, `ETHUSDC`, `BNBUSDC`, `SOLUSDC`, `XRPUSDC`

**Tier 2 — Liquid mid-caps (10):**
`ADAUSDC`, `AVAXUSDC`, `LINKUSDC`, `DOGEUSDC`, `TRXUSDC`, `DOTUSDC`, `POLUSDC`, `LTCUSDC`, `ATOMUSDC`, `UNIUSDC`

**Tier 3 — Higher-vol but liquid (4):**
`NEARUSDC`, `ARBUSDC`, `APTUSDC`, `PEPEUSDC` *(verify USDC depth — keep position size small)*

**Tier 4 — Cross / fiat diversification (1):**
`ETHBTC` *(crypto/crypto, no stablecoin; cointegration anchor and executable)*

**Optional EEA extra:** an `EURI/USDC` or `BTC/EUR` sleeve to tap the EUR-leg zero-fee promo and diversify stablecoin-issuer risk.

**Selection logic and correlation structure.** Every pair clears a liquidity floor adequate for $10K. The cross-section spans L1s (ETH, SOL, ADA, AVAX, BNB, TRX), L2/scaling (POL, ARB), DeFi (LINK, UNI), memes/high-beta (DOGE, PEPE), payments (XRP, LTC), and interoperability (DOT, ATOM, NEAR) — enough dispersion for a ranker. Natural cointegration clusters for the pairs-feature work: ETH–SOL–AVAX (L1s), ADA–DOT–ATOM (alt-L1s), POL–ARB (scaling), DOGE–PEPE (memes). ETH/BTC provides an explicit ratio series.

**Two adjustments versus a USDT basket:** (1) USDC depth on the majors is solid, but on Tier-3 and PEPE it can be materially thinner — the live USDC book should be checked to confirm a $1–2k clip slips under ~10–20 bps, with substitution if not. (2) Several of these (BNB/USDC, ADA/USDC, TRX/USDC, XRP/USDC) have appeared in zero-fee promotions — a bonus aligning with the basket, though the current eligible list should be checked in-account, since it rotates.

## 8. Fees and Execution Economics (VIP2 + BNB + Zero-Fee USDC)<a name="8-fees-and-execution-economics-vip2--bnb--zero-fee-usdc"></a>

VIP2 was reached via the **100-BNB holding path** — a legitimate route, since Binance grants the tier on 30-day volume *or* BNB held (VIP2 = $5M volume **or** 100 BNB; CoinPath, Feb 2026). VIP2 spot fees are **0.0800% maker / 0.1000% taker**. The "−20% Spot Fee" shown in-account is the VIP2 maker reduction (0.08% is exactly 20% below the 0.10% base maker); the *taker* side is unchanged at standard VIP2, so the fee page should be checked to confirm whether the −20% label covers only the maker side or reflects an additional adjustment.

|                                     | Maker   | Taker   | Round-trip (maker→maker) |
| ----------------------------------- | ------- | ------- | ------------------------ |
| VIP2 standard                       | 0.0800% | 0.1000% | 16.0 bps                 |
| VIP2 + 25% BNB deduction            | 0.0600% | 0.0750% | **12.0 bps**             |
| USDC zero-fee promo pair (eligible) | 0.0000% | 0.0000% | **~0 bps**               |

The 25% BNB deduction stacks on the VIP2 rates for normal pairs — the "Pay fees with BNB" option should remain enabled, with a small BNB balance maintained.

**The zero-fee USDC interaction — a real edge, with caveats.** VIP2 status falls within the eligibility band for Binance's recurring zero-fee USDC promotions: zero maker and taker fees for VIP 2–9 users and Spot Liquidity Providers on selected USDC pairs (recent examples: BNB/USDC, ADA/USDC, TRX/USDC, XRP/USDC; Binance announcement, 2025), plus an EEA-specific EUR/USDC zero-fee promotion. MiCA forces the move to USDC; VIP2 permits trading a rotating subset of USDC pairs at zero cost. The qualifications:

1. **It is promotional and rotates.** The eligible pair list changes by announcement, and standard fees resume after each promotion period. Cost modeling should use **12 bps round-trip as the baseline**; zero-fee pairs are a bonus, not a permanent assumption.
1. **The BNB discount does not stack on promo pairs** (already 0%, and the terms exclude it).
1. **Promo-pair volume is excluded from VIP-tier volume calculation** — irrelevant where the tier is held via BNB.
1. **USDT/FDUSD zero-fee promos do not apply** — those stablecoins are not tradeable in the EEA. Only USDC and EUR/EURI promotions are reachable.

**Net effect on a maker-limit, daily-rebalanced strategy:** ~12 bps round-trip on normal USDC pairs, toward zero on current zero-fee majors. At a 300–800 bps mid-cap swing, fees are 1.5–4% of gross — favorable.

## 9. Binance API and Infrastructure for Hours-to-Days<a name="9-binance-api-and-infrastructure-for-hours-to-days"></a>

Rate limits are an HFT concern, not a swing concern — only a small fraction of these limits is used:

- **Spot REST:** ~6,000 request-weight per minute per IP (most market-data calls weight 1–10).
- **Order rate limit:** 100 new orders per 10 seconds, 200,000 per 24 hours per account (raised from 50/10s in late 2023). Notably, *filled* orders do not count against the order-rate limit.
- **WebSocket** for live order books; **REST** for one-shot history and order placement.

Libraries: **python-binance** (Binance-specific, often preferred for a Binance-only production setup) or **CCXT** (unified multi-exchange API; useful even for a single venue, for clean abstractions). For a daily-rebalanced strategy, a single VPS in a region with reasonable latency to Binance is ample; co-location is unnecessary.

## 10. Qlib Adaptation for Crypto (Self-Built Pipeline)<a name="10-qlib-adaptation-for-crypto-self-built-pipeline"></a>

Since the calendar and fetcher are self-built, the limitations of Qlib's bundled CoinGecko crypto collector (data-retrieval-only, no backtest support) are irrelevant. Four elements are required:

1. **A 24/7 continuous calendar (daily).** Crypto has no weekends or market holidays. A `day.txt` with no skipped days is needed. Qlib's default equity calendars and region presets (`REG_US`, `REG_CN`) throw calendar errors on crypto data and should not be used.
1. **Binance OHLCV ingestion → Qlib binary format.** Pull daily klines via python-binance/CCXT, then run them through Qlib's `scripts/dump_bin.py` to produce the binary layout Qlib expects. The history trick applies: ingest **USDT-quoted** daily klines for the long history, model on those, execute on USDC.
1. **A benchmark instrument override.** Point Qlib's backtest at a crypto benchmark (`BTCUSDC`, or an equal-weight basket index) so the engine does not error on a missing equity index.
1. **Correct labeling and CV.** One decision per day; **purged k-fold CV with embargo**; Alpha158 computed on daily bars (its native cadence).

**Reference material:**

- Official Qlib repo — `https://github.com/microsoft/qlib` (~35k stars; the LightGBM Alpha158 workflow config is the canonical entry point).
- Qlib docs — `https://qlib.readthedocs.io/en/latest/`
- Qlib paper — Yang, Liu, Zhou, Bian, Liu, *"Qlib: An AI-oriented Quantitative Investment Platform,"* arXiv **2009.11189** (2020).
- Open Qlib issues on custom datasets and calendars surface the exact errors and workarounds others hit when adapting Qlib to 24/7 markets.
- Community crypto-adjacent projects worth skimming for patterns: **UFund-Me/Qbot** (`https://github.com/UFund-Me/Qbot`), and `github.com/topics/qlib`.
- Microsoft **RD-Agent** (`https://github.com/microsoft/RD-Agent`) for LLM-driven factor mining once the pipeline works.

### (b) AI / ML quantitative trading (general)<a name="b-ai--ml-quantitative-trading-general"></a>

**Books, in priority order:**

1. **Marcos López de Prado, *Advances in Financial Machine Learning*** (Wiley, 2018). The essential reference for *avoiding* the mistakes that kill retail ML strategies: information-driven bars, triple-barrier labeling, sample weights for overlapping observations, fractional differentiation, **purged k-fold CV with embargo**, **combinatorial purged CV (CPCV)**, and the probability-of-backtest-overfitting framework. If only one reference is read, it should be this one.

## 12. Risk Warnings<a name="12-risk-warnings"></a>

- **The base rate for retail algo strategies is loss or buy-and-hold underperformance.** This is the prior, not pessimism.
- **Backtest overfitting is the primary killer.** Testing 20 variants and selecting the best makes an apparent Sharpe of 2.0 likely a true Sharpe under 0.5. Purged k-fold / CPCV, a held-out final out-of-sample window, and fewer trials are the defenses, alongside the deflated Sharpe ratio.
- **Survivorship bias is severe in crypto.** Binance delisted ~49 spot pairs in Q1 2025 alone, with 20–50 per quarter typical in 2024–2025. A universe of "currently listed coins" inflates historical results; a point-in-time universe is required.
- **Low-liquidity USDC depth.** USDC books on smaller names can be thinner than the USDT books the backtest history is drawn from — live depth should be validated before sizing.
- **The institutional-competition concern is misplaced at hours-to-days**, but the execution-and-overfitting concern is real and under-appreciated. Disproportionate effort belongs in time-series cross-validation, realistic cost modeling, and ≥3 months of paper trading before capital is risked.

## 13. Staged Action Plan (Daily Baseline)<a name="13-staged-action-plan-daily-baseline"></a>

**Stage 1 (Weeks 1–4): Infrastructure.**

- Install Qlib; complete the LightGBM Alpha158 tutorial on the bundled equity data first to confirm the toolchain.
- Write the Binance OHLCV fetcher (python-binance/CCXT); pull **daily** bars for the basket coins back to ≥2020 (earlier where available) using **USDT-quoted** history. (Daily bars are the baseline; see Section 14 on optionally archiving finer-grained history now as low-cost insurance.)
- Build the 24/7 daily calendar; dump to Qlib binary; set `BTCUSDC` (or a basket index) as benchmark. Verify a trivial backtest runs.
- *Gate to advance:* a working Qlib backtest of an equal-weight buy-and-hold of the basket, matching a manual pandas computation to within 1 bp.

**Stage 2 (Weeks 5–10): Baseline model.**

- LightGBM ranker on **Alpha158 (daily)** forecasting 1–3-day forward returns, cross-sectional across the 20 coins; **one decision per day**.
- **Purged k-fold CV with embargo.** Top-K long-only, daily rebalance, **costs: 12 bps round-trip + a size-scaled slippage term**.
- *Gate to advance:* net Sharpe > 0.7 out-of-sample over the multi-year window, max drawdown < 35%.

**Stage 3 (Weeks 11–16): Robustness.**

- Re-run with combinatorial purged CV; apply the deflated-Sharpe correction.
- Stress across training-window choices (start 2017 vs. 2020) and through LUNA/FTX.
- Add the BTC-trend regime filter and volatility-targeting overlay.
- Optionally pull **aggTrades** for a sample window to validate slippage and maker-fill assumptions; this becomes essential only if higher frequency is later adopted (Section 14).
- *Gate to advance:* deflated Sharpe > 0.5; robustness across ≥3 training windows.

**Stage 4 (Weeks 17–24): Paper trading.**

- Run live against Binance with paper fills; compare paper P&L to backtest weekly.
- *Gate to advance:* paper P&L tracks backtest within 1 SD over 3 months. **>20% divergence over a quarter ⇒ the backtest is wrong; return to Stage 2.**

**Stage 5 (Month 7+): Live, small size.**

- Deploy $1,000–2,000 of the $10K; park the remainder in USDC or BTC as a benchmark.
- Scale up only after 3 months of live performance matching expectations.

**Thresholds that change the plan:**

- *Switch to simpler trend-following* if ML out-of-sample Sharpe < 0.3 after Stage 3 — complexity is not paying.
- *Add a cointegration-pairs sleeve* if ≥3 stable cointegrating pairs pass rolling Augmented Engle-Granger tests.
- *Abandon and rebuild* if live performance is < 50% of backtest expectations for two consecutive quarters.

## 14. Future Direction — Higher-Frequency (15-Minute) Extension<a name="14-future-direction-%E2%80%94-higher-frequency-15-minute-extension"></a>

This is a deferred roadmap item, not part of the initial build. It is documented so the path is clear once the conditions below are met.

**Intended shape of the extension.** The recommended form is not per-bar trading. It is: **15-minute bars used for features and triple-barrier labels, hours-to-days holding retained, and execution gated to an hourly rebalance with a no-trade band.** The governing principle is to decouple signal frequency from execution frequency — extracting richer information from finer bars while constraining turnover so that fees and slippage do not consume the edge. This captures the real upside of higher resolution (richer features, cross-asset lead-lag, a stronger mean-reversion/pairs sleeve, better entry and exit timing) without sliding into the faster, more crowded regime where execution quality and fee drag dominate and where the institutional-competition concern begins to apply.

**What changes in data preparation.**

- *A 15-minute continuous calendar* (96 slots per day, 24/7, no gaps). Qlib's high-frequency path is less mature than its daily path — calendar handling is where most adaptation effort goes — so `freq` must be set explicitly in both the dump step and the data handler, and the grid verified across all instruments.
- *An explicit gap and ragged-start policy.* At daily resolution, missing bars and differing listing dates largely wash out; at 15-min they do not (maintenance windows, low-trade bars, later-listed coins). A deliberate rule is required — forward-fill price but zero-fill volume, mask, or drop — because volume-based features depend on it.
- *Stricter lookahead discipline.* Klines are UTC and open-timestamped: a bar labeled 12:00 covers 12:00–12:15 and closes at 12:15. At a decision time, only bars that have already closed may be used. This off-by-one error is far easier to commit at 15-min than at daily and silently inflates backtests.
- *Multi-horizon and intraday features.* Alpha158 windows are counted in bars, so they must be computed on bars aggregated to 4h/daily to preserve their slow semantics, supplemented by features specific to intraday data: intraday realized volatility (a superior vol estimator for sizing), time-of-day and day-of-week seasonality (US/EU opens, the 00:00 UTC roll, the 8-hour perpetual-funding timestamps that propagate into spot), cross-asset lead-lag (whether BTC's recent move predicts altcoin moves over the next several bars — a legitimate intraday edge invisible at daily resolution), and bar-shape/signed-volume proxies.
- *Triple-barrier labeling becomes a necessity, not a nicety.* Labeling each 15-min bar with a multi-day forward return creates overlap with ~96–288 neighbors — extreme autocorrelation and leakage. Triple-barrier labeling (profit-take, stop-loss, and time barriers, labeled by first touch) is designed for exactly this: variable holding within an hours-to-days band, observed on fine bars. It pairs with uniqueness-based sample weights to down-weight overlapping labels, and the CV embargo must be sized in bars (a 2-day horizon implies ~192 fifteen-minute bars of embargo).
- *Compute and storage grow roughly 96×.* The panel reaches a few million rows; Qlib's expression engine on full Alpha158 at that scale is slow, so features should be precomputed and cached (for example, to parquet). Critically, more rows are not more independent information: a few million 15-min rows from one year still contain about one regime's worth of independent macro signal, so the 3–5-year history requirement is unchanged and the false sense of statistical comfort is, if anything, more dangerous.

**What changes in strategy and execution.**

- *Turnover and fees become the dominant risk.* Per-bar re-trading compounds even a ~12 bps round-trip (or zero on promo pairs) into a serious drag. The controls are a no-trade band (rebalance only when the target portfolio differs materially from the current one), an explicit turnover penalty in portfolio construction, and an hourly (not per-bar) rebalance clock. Without these, a higher-frequency strategy appears excellent in a frictionless backtest and loses money live.
- *Execution realism stops being optional.* The gap between assumed and actual fills widens at 15-min, and the low/zero-fee economics depend on maker limit orders filling; an unfilled limit order forces either a taker-fee chase or a missed trade. The aggTrades validation deferred at daily frequency becomes essential here, and the slippage model should use intraday liquidity (volume available within a 15-min window) rather than daily average volume.
- *Family-specific effects.* The cross-sectional ML ranker remains the core and benefits from lead-lag and intraday features, but signal-to-noise falls at shorter horizons, making regularization and turnover control decisive. The stat-arb/pairs/mean-reversion sleeve benefits most, as cointegration spreads oscillate intraday in ways daily bars miss — strengthening the case to promote it from a feature to its own component. Momentum remains a regime overlay, with the BTC-trend regime filter computed on a daily clock even while trading on 15-min.
- *Infrastructure* shifts from a daily cron job to a persistent, monitored 24/7 process with restart logic and websocket maintenance. The latency budget remains generous (15 minutes) and rate limits remain distant, so this is an operational-reliability problem, not a speed problem.

**A low-cost hedge available now.** Although the baseline uses daily bars, archiving 15-minute history from the outset is inexpensive insurance: finer bars can always be aggregated to daily, but cannot be reconstructed after the fact. Fetching and storing 15-min klines in parallel with the daily build preserves the option without affecting the initial strategy.

**Trigger — when to investigate this direction.** The extension should not be undertaken until the daily baseline has cleared Stage 4 (paper trading) and is performing live, in line with backtest. It becomes worth investigating when one or more of the following appear:

- The daily strategy's edge is real but capacity- or turnover-constrained, leaving identifiable alpha uncaptured between daily decision points.
- Signal-decay analysis shows the model's alpha decays materially within hours, implying faster reaction would capture more of it before it dissipates.
- The mean-reversion/pairs sleeve exhibits strong intraday spread oscillation that daily bars cannot resolve.
- Drawdowns trace to slow reaction to intraday regime shifts that finer bars would catch earlier.

Absent at least one of these, the additional resolution adds overfitting surface and operational fragility without a corresponding edge.

**Adoption bar.** Even once investigated, adoption requires the 15-minute-feature model to beat the daily baseline on an identical purged-CV protocol, after charging realistic turnover and fees, by a meaningful net-Sharpe margin. If it does not clear that bar, the daily baseline stands and the finer resolution is treated as added overfitting surface to be discarded.

## 15. Caveats and Things That Change<a name="15-caveats-and-things-that-change"></a>

- **Fees, VIP requirements, and zero-fee USDC promotions change periodically.** The VIP2 0.08/0.10 rates, the 25% BNB deduction, and the VIP2–9 zero-fee USDC eligibility are current as of mid-2026, but should be reconfirmed on Binance's fee schedule and announcements before sizing trade economics. Promo pair lists rotate.
- **MiCA enforcement and the authorized-stablecoin set are still evolving.** USDC and the euro tokens are compliant today; the list of authorized issuers updates as more secure (or lose) authorization. USDT's EEA delisting is in force and Tether has signaled no intent to seek MiCA authorization.
- **Jurisdiction is Germany/EEA.** This document assumes Binance-EEA product availability. These figures should not be generalized to non-EEA Binance entities or to binance.us, which differ in listings, fees, and stablecoin availability.
- **Ticker migrations happen** (for example, MATIC → POL). Current Binance tickers should be verified before coding the basket.
- **Academic crypto-factor results were estimated in earlier regimes** (much pre-2022). Out-of-sample regime change is non-trivial.
- **"Most retail algo strategies lose money"** is a well-supported statistical generalization. Outcomes depend on discipline, infrastructure quality, and luck. The first year is best treated as an education expense, not an investment.
- **This is not financial, legal, or tax advice.** It is a research and engineering roadmap. Regulatory and tax specifics should be confirmed with qualified professionals.
